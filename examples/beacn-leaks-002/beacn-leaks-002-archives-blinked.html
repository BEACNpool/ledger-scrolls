THE YEAR THE
ARCHIVES BLINKED

BEACN LEAKS · ISSUE 002 · JULY 2026

In eighteen months, the world was reminded that the public record is a service — and services have owners, budgets, and off switches. This document assembles what happened, with sources, and stores itself where none of it can happen to this page.

I. WHAT WAS REMOVED

FACT. Beginning January 2025, United States federal agencies removed or modified more than 8,000 public web pages and approximately 3,000 public datasets — health research, environmental data, demographic statistics.
source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_United_States_government_online_resource_removals
FACT. In February 2026 the U.S. State Department removed its social-media posts published before January 20, 2025 from public view. The posts were archived internally — accessible to the government, not to you.
source: reported in the chronology above; see also nsarchive.gwu.edu "A Disappearing Data Chronology"
FACT. The CIA withdrew 17 previously published intelligence assessments after an internal review.
source: notus.org, "Federal Data Is Disappearing"

None of this required burning anything. A web page is withdrawn the way a courtesy is withdrawn — quietly, completely, and with no obligation to explain. Whoever owns the medium owns the memory.

II. THE BACKUP BLINKED

The world's answer to link rot has been one nonprofit: the Internet Archive's Wayback Machine, which passed one trillion archived pages in early 2026. A staggering achievement — and a single point of failure with a postal address.

FACT. By early 2026, major news organizations — among them The Guardian and The New York Times — began blocking the Wayback Machine from archiving their pages, because AI companies were using archived copies to route around scraping blocks. The archive of record is now partly opt-out.
source: eff.org, "Blocking the Internet Archive Won't Stop AI, But It Will Erase the Web's Historical Record" (2026)
FACT. Volunteer efforts — the Data Rescue Project, Harvard's Library Innovation Lab (311,000 datasets copied) — raced to mirror what governments were removing. Heroic, necessary, and funded by goodwill, which is to say: temporary.
source: nsarchive.gwu.edu; lil.law.harvard.edu
Every archive you have ever trusted shares one property: someone can be ordered, paid, pressured, or simply tired enough to stop. The record survives at the pleasure of its custodian.

III. A RECORD WITH NO CUSTODIAN

This document is a Ledger Scroll. Its bytes live inside Cardano transactions, replicated by thousands of independent nodes whose operators could not alter it if they wanted to and could not be ordered to, because no one of them holds the original. There is no original. There are only copies, everywhere, verified by arithmetic on every read.

It cannot be blocked from archiving itself. It cannot be pressured by a publisher. It does not depend on a donation drive. It cost a few dollars, once, and it will outlive every institution named above.

We did not build this to replace the archivists — we built it so the things that matter most have somewhere to stand when the archives blink. The Data Rescue volunteers mirror data onto servers; a scroll puts it where removal is not an operation that exists.
